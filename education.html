Learning at the Zoo

Enhance your learning journey with our immersive zoo programs, from interactive workshops and curriculum-aligned school visits to expert-led conservation training sessions. Discover fascinating animal behaviors, explore diverse habitats, and gain valuable insights into wildlife protection through activities designed for students, educators, and nature enthusiasts alike.

School Programs

Our school programs provide engaging, hands-on learning experiences tailored for students of all ages. Through a variety of activities, students can explore animal behavior, understand ecosystems, and learn about conservation efforts while connecting classroom knowledge with real-world experiences at the zoo.


  • Guided Field Trips:

    Discover an immersive educational experience led by trained wildlife educators. Students explore major exhibits, observe animals up close, and participate in hands-on activities such as examining animal artifacts, identifying habitats, and completing fun learning challenges. Some programs include behind-the-scenes access, live demonstrations, and curriculum-based lessons on ecosystems, adaptations, endangered species, and conservation efforts. Ideal for school groups seeking an interactive way to connect with nature and deepen their understanding of wildlife.


  • Classroom Kits:

    Access thoughtfully designed educational materials that help teachers prepare students for their zoo visit or reinforce lessons afterwards. Each kit includes teacher guides, printable activity sheets, science-based worksheets, educational videos, discussion prompts, hands-on project ideas, vocabulary lists, and assessment tools. The resources align with curriculum standards and support topics such as animal behavior, ecosystems, adaptations, and conservation.


  • Virtual Zoo Lessons:

    Experience the zoo from anywhere through live online sessions led by expert educators and animal caretakers. These interactive lessons include behind-the-scenes virtual tours, close-up animal encounters, live demonstrations, and real-time Q&A. Sessions are curriculum-aligned, offering students an engaging way to explore wildlife, ecosystems, adaptations, and conservation. Teachers also receive supporting materials such as worksheets and discussion guides to extend learning beyond the virtual visit.

Workshops & Camps

Fun and educational activities designed for young explorers.


  • Summer Camps: Week-long animal and nature-themed camps:-

    Woodland Park Zoo offers weekly summer-day camps during the school break. The camps are designed to connect children with nature, wildlife, and conservation — using a mix of science learning, games, exploration, and fun. The camp curricula are aligned to recognized educational standards (for example, the Next Generation Science Standards — NGSS), making them more than just “zoo-fun”: they aim to deliver meaningful science- and nature-based learning for kids.


  • Weekend Workshops:

    Under their “Adult Programs / Programs & Classes” they list things like art-and-nature classes: for example a workshop called Forest Watercolors with Willow Heath (held on a Saturday). The Zoo’s public events calendar also lists occasional “workshop / public event / family-friendly event” slots. There have been external-partnered events — for example, a past event described as Art with a Heart: Interactive Workshops with Woodland Park Zoo: a family-oriented workshop blending wildlife education with art & crafts.


  • Teen Volunteer Program:- What is the Teen Volunteer / Ambassador Program: The zoo offers a Teen Ambassador Volunteer Program for high-school–aged students interested in wildlife, conservation, and public engagement. Through this program, teens help the zoo by: Volunteering in spaces like the zoo’s indoor nature play area (called “Zoomazium”). Helping in exhibits such as the zoo’s “Reptile Realm,” where they share information about reptiles and inspire guests to care about conservation. Observing and collecting data about animal behavior or how guests interact with exhibits — contributing to the zoo’s scientific / conservation efforts. Participating in weekly teen-meetings: a mix of conservation-career exploration, leadership development, public speaking, and teamwork. It’s not a program for “hands-on” animal care: teen volunteers do not work directly with animals or do behind-the-scenes animal care. Their role is more about guest engagement, education, outreach, and observational support.

Conservation Education

Inspiring visitors to protect wildlife and the environment.


  • Woodland Park Zoo’s “Learning & Innovation” department explicitly aims to “inspire and cultivate an informed community of conservation stewards.” Their educational programs are built around a science- and outcomes-based framework — not just showing animals, but fostering empathy, knowledge, and action toward conservation.
  • The philosophy is: connecting people (from toddlers to seniors) to wildlife and nature — so that they understand why conservation matters, feel emotionally connected, and take responsibility for protecting ecosystems.
  • The Zoo doesn’t treat education as just awareness or entertainment — it's part of a broader conservation mission. Their on-site education is tightly linked to real-world field-conservation programs: the Zoo supports projects ranging from species recovery in the Pacific Northwest to habitat protection globally.
  • Through its educational offerings, the Zoo aims to not just inform, but empower — giving individuals the knowledge, empathy, and tools to take conservation actions, support wildlife beyond the zoo, and contribute to long-term ecological health.

Animal Encounters

Special learning sessions where visitors can meet animals up close.


  • Keeper Talks: Woodland Park Zoo schedules “Animal Keeper Talks” every day (roughly 10:30 a.m.–3:00 p.m., with times and species varying) as part of its daily program. These are public presentations by zoo keepers — usually near a particular exhibit (e.g. Brown Bear, Jaguar, Rhino, Wolves, Red Panda, Kea, etc.) depending on the day. The talks are free with zoo admission and meant to give visitors insight into the animals’ lives, care, and behaviors.

  • Feeding Sessions: Watch animals enjoy their meals.

  • Behind-the-Scenes Tours: The zoo has offered special guided tours that go beyond the public exhibit paths — under names like “Real Close” tours or “Eye-to-Eye / Behind-the-Scenes” tours. These tours gave participants a chance to enter areas normally restricted to general visitors — e.g. animal-care zones, commissary (where animal food is prepared), behind locked-gates, and other “staff only” parts of the zoo.

  • The tours could include: Visiting the zoo’s central operations/food prep / “behind-the-scenes” zones. Getting a close-up look at some animal exhibits from unique vantage points (for example, a closer view of savanna animals, or a different perspective on the zoo’s African Savanna). A visit to the zoo’s greenhouses (where many of the plants for the exhibits are grown), showing the horticulture aspect of what makes the zoo’s habitats possible. ParentMap A “Real Close” walk through exhibits like the penguin house or Tropical Rainforest, including a look “behind the gate” at mechanical or maintenance aspects of exhibits.

Science & Research

Discover how the zoo helps advance global wildlife knowledge.


  • Field Conservation & Species-Survival Programs:- Woodland Park Zoo runs a program called the Wildlife Survival Program — it funds and supports field-conservation projects globally, for threatened species that the Zoo may house or support. Their conservation portfolio spans many regions and species — from big mammals to reptiles, aquatic species, and even invertebrates. Through these efforts, the Zoo helps maintain biodiversity, support wild populations, and fund research, habitat protection, and species recovery.

  • Signature & Long-Term Research Programs:- A major example: the Tree Kangaroo Conservation Program (TKCP). This program studies ecology, behavior, habitat use, and conservation management of a threatened species — the Matschie’s tree kangaroo — in Papua New Guinea. Research under TKCP involves advanced tools: for instance, “Crittercams” (animal-mounted cameras) to study elusive tree-kangaroos in their natural habitat (in cloud-forest canopy) across elevations. This helps inform habitat-management decisions and conservation strategies. The Zoo — by supporting such field research — acts not only as a site for captive-animal care but as a source of scientific data and conservation biology research applied in the wild.

  • Local / Regional Ecology & Community Science:- Through the Living Northwest initiative, Woodland Park Zoo works on species and ecosystem conservation in its home region (Pacific Northwest). As part of that, the Zoo runs a formal Community Science arm — public volunteers help monitor local wildlife. Programs include amphibian monitoring (wetlands), bat-activity surveys, tracking bobcats/coyotes/urban carnivores, etc. This gives non-scientists a chance to contribute to real data gathering, and helps the Zoo map species distributions, behavior, habitat use, and responses to environmental changes in the wild.
